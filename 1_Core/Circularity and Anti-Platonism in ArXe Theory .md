# Circularity and Anti-Platonism in ArXe Theory  
### A Reconfiguration of Ontological Foundations

## Introduction

ArXe theory proposes an ontological reconfiguration that directly addresses two central issues in philosophy of science: circularity and Platonism. The key to this reformulation lies in a strict distinction between essence, existence, and truth. By separating these domains, contradiction can possess formal status without constituting an existing entity or a true proposition. Within this framework, circularity is not a logical defect but a structural mechanism of stabilization, and no ultimate positive ontological foundation is required. ArXe does not replace Platonism with a negative absolute; rather, it replaces transcendent foundations with temporal structural stabilization without prior ideal entities.

---

## 1. Breaking the Classical Co-Implication

Much of classical metaphysics—and the ontological background presupposed by modern science—operates under a structural co-implication between:

- Being  
- Existence  
- Truth  
- Non-contradiction  

What is, in some relevant sense, exists and is susceptible to truth. Contradiction, accordingly, lacks ontological status: if something is contradictory, it is false; if false, it cannot ground anything.

ArXe breaks this co-implication.

The theory formally distinguishes:

- Essence (being)  
- Existence  
- Truth  

Thus, something may have formal status without existing or being true. This separation is the necessary condition for redefining both contradiction and circularity.

---

## 2. The Formal Status of Contradiction

In ArXe, contradiction (S ∧ ¬S):

- Does not exist  
- Is not true  
- Is not a physical fact  
- Has no temporality or localization  

It is not an ontological principle nor a universal foundation. It is merely a formal configuration of indeterminacy.

Its “being” is neither existence nor truth, but formal status. Consequently, contradiction cannot stabilize as a determinate fact.

This is decisive: contradiction does not ground, explain, or necessitate anything. It simply cannot constitute a stable determination.

---

## 3. Circularity as Structural Closure

In classical logic, circularity is an argumentative defect. In ArXe, by contrast, circularity is structural.

Formal indeterminacy is not eliminated through falsity; it is integrated through closure. The theory refers to the stratification of closure levels as *n-arity*, whereby indeterminacy is absorbed without collapsing into triviality.

Circularity here does not imply self-justification or logical presupposition. It indicates that certain regimes of determination stabilize through structural return.

Thus:

- There is no petitio principii.  
- There is no tautological self-grounding.  
- There is no transcendent external foundation.  

There is structural stabilization.

---

## 4. Exentation and the Fundamental Act

The negation of contradiction, ¬(S ∧ ¬S), is formally valid. However, logical validity does not entail ontological necessity.

Exentation designates the first regime of stabilization. With it, temporality emerges. The fundamental act:

- Is temporal  
- Is non-spatial  
- Is not an ideal entity  
- Is not an eternal truth  
- Is not a positive ontological affirmation  

There is no prior domain in which forms or principles reside. Space emerges later as a structural effect.

If one speaks of a foundation at all, it is not an entity or truth but a minimal temporal act of stabilization.

---

## 5. Structural Anti-Platonism

Ontological Platonism posits an ultimate positive foundation: eternal truth, ideal form, or structure independent of process.

ArXe posits none of the following:

- A transcendent domain  
- An original eternal truth  
- An ungenerated ideal entity  
- An absolute ontological principle  

Contradiction cannot fulfill this role, as it lacks existence and truth. The fundamental act cannot fulfill it either, as it is temporal and non-spatial rather than eternal and ideal.

ArXe therefore does not replace Platonism with a negative absolute. It removes the necessity of any transcendent ontological foundation.

---

## Conclusion

ArXe redefines circularity and the problem of foundations by distinguishing essence, existence, and truth. Contradiction acquires formal status without becoming an entity; circularity becomes a mechanism of structural stabilization; foundation ceases to be transcendent and becomes a minimal temporal act.

Accordingly, ArXe is not Platonist in any strong ontological sense. It proposes an ontology of stabilization without a positive metaphysical absolute.
